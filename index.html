Product Requirements Document

Competition Filter:
surface the roles you can actually win

Jobright tells you how well you match a job. It doesn't tell you how many people you're up against. This feature closes that gap.

Product
Jobright · Job Feed
Type
New feature

01 · PROBLEM

Match score is absolute. Job hunting is competitive.

Jobright shows users how well they match a role, but not how contested it is. Users can't see or filter by applicant volume — so they spend limited application time on high-match but heavily contested roles, while overlooking high-match roles with far fewer applicants.

Here are two real roles from the same feed, shown side by side with no way to tell which is the smarter bet:

Snowflake
Product Finance Analyst
Match78%
Applicants200+
High competition
Lower fit, 200+ applicants — your application is one of hundreds.
SMARTER BET
Itron, Inc.
FP&A Associate
Match94%
Applicants<25
Low competition
Higher fit, under 25 applicants — far better odds of being seen.

The Itron role is both a better fit and far less contested — yet the feed gives no way to prioritize by competition.

02 · COMPETITIVE CONTEXT

LinkedIn proves the need — but only does half of it

LinkedIn's "Under 10 applicants" filter is widely cited as one of its most valuable job-search filters, confirming that competition is a dimension users actively want. But LinkedIn treats fit and competition as separate signals, and puts deeper applicant insights behind a paid tier.

Jobright has a structural advantage LinkedIn lacks: a match score on every role. That lets Jobright do what LinkedIn can't — combine fit and competition into one view.

LinkedIn today
Jobright's opportunity
One fixed threshold ("Under 10")
Multiple tiers — under 25 / 50 / 100
Fit & competition shown separately
Combined into one "best bet" view
Deeper insight is paywalled
Built on data already in the feed
03 · WHY IT MATTERS

For high-volume applicants, competition is the missing signal

A high match score reflects fit, but not a candidate's odds of being noticed. For new grads and entry-level users applying at volume with limited time, a 94% match with 20 applicants is a far better use of effort than a 78% match with 200+.

This serves Jobright's own promise. The product already pledges to "save 80% of job search time" and surface "only roles you can win." Making competition visible is a direct expression of that mission.

04 · TARGET USER

Who this is for

Primary

New grads and entry-level job seekers (0–2 years) applying at high volume with limited time — the users most hurt by wasting applications on contested roles, and Jobright's core early-career segment.

Secondary

Career switchers who match well on transferable skills but need to be strategic about where they spend effort.

05 · SOLUTION

Make competition a first-class signal

Built from applicant-volume data Jobright already displays. Three connected pieces, shipped in priority order:

Ship first Filter

Competition filter

Filter the feed by applicant volume across multiple tiers — Under 25 / 50 / 100 — instead of one fixed threshold. Solves the core need with the least engineering, since the data and filter system already exist.

Label

Competition indicator

On each job card, a simple Low / Medium / High competition tag next to the match score, so users can scan it at a glance without opening filters.

Sort

"Best Bet" sort

A sort that ranks roles by combining high match score with low competition — surfacing the highest-value applications first.

Why filter first? It's the core of the feature, the cheapest to build on existing data, and the fastest way to validate the need. If usage and callback data prove it out, label and sort follow.

06 · HOW IT WORKS

One user, one search

Henry opens his feed, filtered to Financial Analyst · New Grad · US.
He applies Competition filter → Under 50 applicants. The 200+ applicant roles drop out of view.
Each remaining card shows both signals — the Itron role reads 94% match · Low competition.
He switches sort to "Best Bet": high-match, low-competition roles rise to the top.
He spends his limited time on the roles where he has the best realistic odds — instead of applying broadly and hoping.
07 · SUCCESS METRICS

How we'd know it works

Adoption

25%+ of weekly active job seekers apply the Competition filter at least once within 2 weeks of launch.

Behavior change

Among filter users, the average applicant-count of roles they apply to drops versus non-users.

Outcome — the one that matters

Measurably higher interview / callback rate for applications to low-competition roles. This is the whole point: time spent where odds are better.

Business

Does landing interviews faster lift retention and free-to-paid conversion? A user who succeeds stays and recommends.

08 · RISKS & OPEN QUESTIONS

What could go wrong

⚠Data accuracy

Applicant counts may lag or be inaccurate, mislabeling a contested role as "low competition."

→ Mitigation: show ranges ("Under 50"), not false-precision exact numbers, and timestamp the data.

⚠Herd effect

If everyone filters for low-competition roles, those roles become high-competition. Open question: does surfacing them erode the advantage over time?

⚠Match-score dependency

"Best Bet" sort is only as reliable as the match score behind it. Noisy match scores weaken the combined signal.

⚠Discouraging reach applications

Some strong candidates should apply to competitive roles. The feature is about awareness, not avoidance — it shouldn't scare users off ambitious bets.
